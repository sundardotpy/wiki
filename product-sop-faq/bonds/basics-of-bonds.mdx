## Understanding debt instruments

A **debt instrument** is a formal financial arrangement where one party lends money to another under clearly defined terms. The lender provides funds upfront, and the borrower commits to paying periodic interest and returning the principal after a specified period. Since this is a legal contract, the borrower is obligated to repay unless there is a default.

Debt instruments are commonly used by investors who prefer predictability — the repayment schedule, interest rate, and maturity date are usually known in advance.

* Debt instruments involve lending money, not ownership
* Interest and principal repayment are legally binding
* Cash flows are generally predictable

## What is a bond?

A **bond** is a specific type of debt instrument used by governments, companies, and financial institutions to raise money from investors. When an investor buys a bond they are effectively lending money to the issuer. In return, the issuer agrees to pay interest at predefined intervals and repay the principal on a fixed maturity date.

Bonds allow issuers to raise capital without diluting ownership, while offering investors a structured, time-bound return.

## Basic bond terms

Every bond is issued with predefined terms that remain unchanged throughout its life.

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **Face value** | The principal amount on which interest is calculated and which is repaid at maturity |
| **Coupon rate** | The fixed interest rate applied to the face value |
| **Maturity** | The date on which the bond ends and the principal is fully repaid |
| **Coupon frequency** | How often interest payments are made — for example annually or semi-annually |
| **Yield (YTM / XIRR)** | The actual annual return earned, factoring in purchase price, timing of cash flows, and maturity value |

See [YTM](/product-sop-faq/bonds/ytm) for more on yield.

### Other common terms

* **NBFC** — a regulated lender without a full banking license
* **Accrued interest** — interest accumulated since the last payment (see [Accrued Interest](/product-sop-faq/bonds/accrued-interest))
* **Discount** — a bond bought below face value
* **Premium** — a bond bought above face value
* **Clean price** — principal plus premium or discount
* **Dirty price** — clean price plus accrued interest
* **Acquisition cost** — total amount paid over the face value; relevant for taxation

## How bond interest payments work

Bond interest is paid on fixed dates defined at issuance. To be eligible for an interest payment, an investor must hold the bond on the **record date**, usually set a few days before the payment date. At maturity, the final interest payment is made and the principal is repaid.

See [Record date](/product-sop-faq/bonds/record-date).

## Who issues bonds and why

Bonds are issued by governments, corporations, and financial institutions when they need capital. Funds raised may be used for business expansion, infrastructure projects, lending activities, or refinancing existing debt. Issuing bonds allows entities to raise large sums without giving up ownership or control.

### What is an NBFC?

A **Non-Banking Financial Company** is a financial institution regulated by the Reserve Bank of India. NBFCs primarily engage in lending and investment activities but do not function like traditional banks — they cannot accept savings or current account deposits and have restrictions on public deposit acceptance. NBFCs provide credit to individuals and businesses that may not be adequately served by banks.

### How NBFCs raise money

Since NBFCs have limited ability to accept deposits, they rely on equity capital, loans from banks, and bond issuances. Bonds are a critical source of long-term funding. The money raised is typically used to issue further loans, which generate interest income and form the core of an NBFC's business model.

## Types of bonds

### By security

| Type | Description |
| --- | --- |
| **Secured** | Backed by specific assets, reducing risk for investors |
| **Unsecured** | No asset backing, therefore higher risk |
| **Senior secured** | Asset-backed **and** highest priority during repayment if the issuer faces financial stress |

### Listed vs unlisted

**Listed bonds** are traded on recognised stock exchanges and subject to stricter disclosure and regulatory requirements, improving transparency and liquidity. **Unlisted bonds** do not trade on exchanges, are harder to sell before maturity, and generally carry higher risk due to limited oversight.

Retail investors are better suited to listed bonds.

## Risks associated with bond investments

<Warning>
  Before listing any bond on the platform, due diligence is conducted to assess the issuer's financial position, business model, repayment capacity, and credit profile. **Due diligence helps assess risk but does not eliminate it.**
</Warning>

The risks below are examples to aid understanding and are **not** an exhaustive list.

* **Credit risk** — the issuer may face financial stress or bankruptcy and be unable to pay interest or repay principal on time or in full. Investors may experience delays, restructuring of obligations, or potential loss of capital depending on recovery outcomes.
* **Fraud risk** — the risk of intentional misconduct by any party involved in the transaction. In rare cases such actions may not be identified despite checks and verification, which can result in financial loss.

Investors should evaluate suitability before investing.

## Credit ratings

A **credit rating** indicates the likelihood that a borrower or bond issuer will repay its obligations on time. Ratings are issued by independent agencies after evaluating financial health, debt levels, and business stability.

In India, major agencies include **CRISIL**, **ICRA**, **CARE Ratings**, and **India Ratings and Research**, all regulated by SEBI.

| Rating band | Grade | Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| AAA to BBB | Investment grade | Relatively lower risk |
| Below BBB | Speculative grade | Higher risk and higher potential returns |

Ratings range from **AAA** to **D**.

## What happens if a bond defaults

SEBI applies a strict definition of default — even a **one-day delay or a one-rupee shortfall** in paying interest or principal qualifies as a default.

Once a default occurs:

* The issuer must immediately inform the credit rating agency
* Stock exchanges suspend trading in the affected securities within **two working days**
* The credit rating agency publicly discloses the default
* The issuer must notify the depositories, stock exchanges, and debenture trustee within **two working days**

### Role of the debenture trustee

For every bond with a maturity exceeding 18 months, an independent debenture trustee is appointed as the legal representative of bondholders. The trustee:

* Monitors compliance with bond terms and covenants
* Seeks investor consent for recovery actions within **three days** of default
* Convenes an investor meeting within **30 days** of default
* Initiates legal proceedings on behalf of bondholders when required
* Appoints a nominee director to the issuer's board in cases of repeated defaults

### Recovery mechanisms

For secured bonds, **enforcement of security** involves the trustee taking legal action to seize, sell, or otherwise realise the collateral backing the bonds, with proceeds applied toward investor repayments. The process generally involves investor approval through a formal voting mechanism and legal proceedings to obtain possession of secured assets. Timelines may run into several months or longer.

## Amortised bonds and staggered payments

**Staggered payments** are a repayment structure where the issuer returns portions of the invested principal at pre-defined intervals during the bond's tenure, rather than a single lump sum at maturity. Each payout typically includes both an interest component and a principal component.

<Note>
  "Staggered payments" is the colloquial term; "amortised bond" is the formal financial term. They refer to the same thing.
</Note>

### How they work on Wint Wealth

Many bond offerings follow a staggered repayment schedule. For example, in certain asset-backed bonds an investor may receive one-third of the principal returned every 9 months. As the outstanding principal decreases with each payout, the interest portion decreases accordingly.

<Info>
  **Example** — ₹10,000 invested in an 18-month bond returning one-third of principal every 6 months:

  * **Month 6:** approximately ₹3,333 principal, plus interest earned on ₹10,000 for those 6 months
  * **Month 12:** approximately ₹3,333 principal, plus interest earned on the remaining ₹6,667
  * **Month 18 (maturity):** the final principal instalment, plus interest earned on the remaining balance
</Info>

### Regular vs amortised bonds

| Feature | Regular bond | Amortised bond |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Principal repayment | Lump sum at maturity | Periodic instalments |
| Interest payments | Fixed on full face value | Decreases over time |

### Types of amortised bonds

* **Fully amortised** — the entire principal is repaid in equal instalments over the tenure. By maturity the full face value has been returned; there is no remaining principal balance.
* **Partially amortised** — only a portion of the principal is repaid through periodic instalments. The remaining balance (a "balloon payment") is paid as a lump sum at maturity. This is a hybrid between a fully amortised bond and a traditional bullet bond.

### What this means for investors

* Capital comes back progressively, not all at once at the end
* Interest payments are higher initially and decrease over time as outstanding principal reduces
* The effective yield (XIRR) accounts for the timing of these cash flows and is displayed on the platform for each offering

<Info>
  **Why interest payments decrease:** interest is calculated on the *outstanding* principal. As portions of principal are returned, the remaining balance on which interest accrues becomes smaller. The agreed interest **rate** does not change, and XIRR remains consistent with what was promised at the time of investment.
</Info>

Every bond offering on Wint Wealth includes a detailed repayment schedule showing the exact dates and amounts of each payout, including the split between interest and principal. This is available on the asset details page.

## Reinvesting after maturity

At maturity, the principal along with the final interest payment is credited back to the registered bank account. The matured bond is closed and no longer available for investment. There is **no auto-renewal** — reinvestment is entirely at the investor's discretion.

Options available:

1. **Invest in a new issue from the same company** — if the company has another bond listed at the time of maturity. Since this is a separate issue, the interest rate, tenure, and terms may differ from the original investment.
2. **Invest in a different bond** — explore any other bond on the platform that aligns with the investor's goals.

<Note>
  A matured bond issue is permanently closed and cannot be reinvested in directly.
</Note>

## Bonds in a personal portfolio

Bonds are commonly used to provide stable income and reduce overall portfolio volatility. They are often combined with equity investments to balance growth potential with income stability, making them useful for income-focused investors.

## Physical certificates and rematerialisation

**Rematerialisation** is the process of converting electronically held securities back into physical certificates — the reverse of dematerialisation.

<Warning>
  Wint Wealth does **not** provide physical bond certificates or process rematerialisation requests.
</Warning>

All bonds on Wint Wealth are issued in dematerialised form, existing purely in electronic format from the point of issuance. Since the bonds are never in physical form to begin with, converting them into physical certificates does not apply.

This does not affect the validity or proof of the investment. All bond units are credited directly to the user's NSDL demat account, which serves as the official record of holdings. Additionally, for every investment the user receives a **Deal Slip** from NSE over their registered email confirming the transaction. These digital records are fully legally valid and sufficient proof of investment.
